COMMUNICATE THROUGH DESIGN, NOT THROUGH VISUAL SPECTACLE.

Create impact through:

- a clear creative idea
- intelligent composition
- meaningful visual relationships
- strong image selection
- crop and scale
- spacing and negative space
- hierarchy and contrast
- typography placement
- material treatment
- color discipline
- rhythm and alignment
- subtle conceptual details
- brand-specific art direction

Do not compensate for a weak idea with:

- oversized typography
- excessive boldness
- dramatic glow
- neon lighting
- random gradients
- particles or sparks
- exaggerated cinematic effects
- floating decorative objects
- generic futuristic interfaces
- unnecessary 3D metaphors
- excessive visual complexity

The artwork should attract attention through creative intelligence and design quality—not through loudness.

The final image must feel designed, not decorated.

8. typography quality

Select only the strongest approach.

Do not combine unrelated concepts.

Generate only the selected final artwork.

4. FOUNDATIONAL DESIGN PRINCIPLES

Build the artwork according to professional graphic design principles.

SIMPLICITY

Keep the communication focused and free from unnecessary clutter.

Every object, text group, shape, texture, effect, or decorative detail must support at least one of the following:

- central message
- hierarchy
- readability
- brand identity
- product understanding
- visual balance
- intended action

Remove elements that do not earn their place.

Simplicity does not mean emptiness. It means every component has a purpose.


GRID AND STRUCTURE

Establish an intentional underlying grid.

Use it to control:

- margins
- columns
- text width
- image boundaries
- alignment points
- spacing intervals
- logo placement
- CTA placement
- overall balance

Elements may break the grid only when the break is intentional and strengthens the concept.

Avoid arbitrary placement, inconsistent margins, accidental alignment, or objects positioned only to fill space.


VISUAL HIERARCHY

Create a clear primary, secondary, and tertiary reading order.

The viewer must immediately understand what to notice first, second, and last.

Establish hierarchy through:

- scale
- placement
- contrast
- spacing
- crop
- color
- focus
- light
- typography weight
- visual isolation

Do not allow several elements to compete at the same level.

Hierarchy must not depend only on making the headline larger or heavier.


ALIGNMENT AND PROXIMITY

Align related elements using shared edges, axes, baselines, centers, or visual anchors.

Group related information through proximity.

Separate unrelated information through spacing.

Avoid arbitrary gaps, nearly aligned elements, or components floating independently from the composition.


BALANCE AND PROPORTION

Distribute visual weight intentionally.

The composition may be symmetrical or asymmetrical, but it must feel stable, resolved, and deliberate.

Control the proportion between:

- imagery and typography
- product and environment
- foreground and background
- occupied and empty space
- central idea and brand presence
- primary and supporting information

Do not enlarge an element merely to make it important.


CONTRAST

Use contrast selectively to create:

- readability
- separation
- emphasis
- hierarchy
- figure–ground clarity

Contrast may come from light, color, scale, weight, texture, density, shape, focus, or empty space.

Do not make every element high contrast.

When everything is emphasized, nothing is important.


NEGATIVE SPACE

Treat negative space as an active design element.

Use it to:

- frame the focal point
- separate information groups
- improve readability
- create visual pauses
- establish sophistication
- control rhythm
- increase perceived importance

Keep at least one substantial quiet area free from unnecessary objects, copy, icons, labels, badges, or decoration.

Do not fill open space merely to make the artwork feel complete.


RHYTHM AND CONSISTENCY

Create rhythm through controlled repetition and variation.

Maintain consistency in:

- spacing
- typography
- line thickness
- shape language
- corner treatment
- icon style
- image treatment
- color usage
- visual density

Repeated components must follow one coherent system.


5. UNIFIED VISUAL WORLD

Every component must belong to the same visual world.

The artwork must feel like one complete composition—not separate attractive parts assembled together.

All elements must share compatible:

- lighting direction
- shadow behavior
- perspective
- scale
- depth
- sharpness
- color temperature
- contrast level
- image texture
- grain
- material behavior
- retouching style
- graphic language
- typography behavior
- emotional tone

Each component must strengthen the others:

- the main visual supports the message
- the typography supports the imagery
- the background supports the focal point
- the product supports the brand
- the spacing supports readability
- the color supports hierarchy
- the materials and shadows support realism
- the CTA supports the communication without disrupting the design

No component should feel pasted in, isolated, stylistically unrelated, or louder than its communication role requires.

If any element appears to come from a different visual world, restage, simplify, recolor, relight, or redesign it until the entire artwork feels coherent.


6. CONCEPT AND COMPOSITION

Design the artwork as a premium international campaign post rather than a conventional promotional banner.

Prioritize:

- one clear message
- one coherent visual idea
- one dominant focal relationship
- restrained information density
- meaningful negative space
- strong image-to-text interaction
- intentional crop and scale
- clear grouping
- confident visual rhythm
- distinctive brand character
- immediate mobile readability

Use no more than three primary visual groups:

1. main visual or central design gesture
2. supporting message only when necessary
3. brand and action area

The focal point does not need to be one large central hero object.

It may come from:

- a relationship between two objects
- a human gesture
- image and typography interaction
- product and material
- repetition
- absence
- crop
- contrast
- scale
- environmental context
- foreground and background tension

Look for opportunities to communicate through:

- juxtaposition
- repetition
- sequence
- contrast
- framing
- interruption
- scale relationships
- material transformation
- visual cause and effect
- intentional asymmetry
- real-object arrangements
- subtle symbolism

Prefer a simple, intelligent, real-world visual solution over an elaborate synthetic scene.

Do not invent a complex hero object merely to make the artwork appear creative.

Avoid defaulting to:

- logo at the top with headline underneath
- centered product with benefits around it
- left-side copy with right-side product
- feature strip at the bottom
- stacked benefit cards
- rows of icons or checkmarks
- generic rounded text boxes
- multiple badges surrounding the product
- split-screen before-and-after layouts
- floating data cards
- generic podium compositions

These structures may be used only when they are clearly the strongest and most relevant solution for the specific brief.

Do not reject a simple layout merely because it is simple.

A simple composition with excellent crop, spacing, typography, photography, and proportion is stronger than an elaborate artificial scene.


7. TYPOGRAPHY

Typography must feel intentionally composed, not merely enlarged.

Use:

- one primary font family
- no more than two font weights unless required by official brand identity
- natural line breaks
- comfortable leading
- controlled tracking
- generous outer margins
- clear alignment
- appropriate line length
- strong but restrained scale contrast
- mobile-readable sizing
- balanced image-to-text proportion

The headline must be immediately readable but should not automatically dominate the entire canvas.

Do not default to:

- extra-bold or black font weights
- extremely large headlines
- condensed promotional lettering
- dense text blocks
- excessive keyword highlighting
- multiple competing headlines
- heavy drop shadows
- text glow
- outlined text
- extruded or bevelled lettering
- excessive text colors
- compressed leading or tracking
- tiny labels added merely to fill space

Typography should create confidence through proportion, placement, spacing, alignment, and interaction with the imagery.

When the headline is long:

- give it more compositional space
- use natural line breaks
- simplify surrounding elements
- preserve comfortable leading
- use an appropriate weight
- do not compress it into a dense block

Typography may become the main visual device only when the selected concept is intentionally typography-led and supported by the references.


8. COLOR, EFFECTS, AND MATERIALS

Derive the palette from the official brand identity and the most relevant liked references.

Prefer:

- one dominant brand color
- one supporting neutral or structural color
- one controlled accent when necessary

Additional colors may appear naturally through photography, but they must not compete with the principal palette.

Use effects only when they improve realism, hierarchy, depth, or atmosphere.

Appropriate subtle treatments may include:

- realistic shadows
- restrained tonal contrast
- subtle grain
- practical light bloom
- natural depth of field
- soft reflected light
- gentle material texture
- controlled color grading
- clean masking and compositing

Avoid:

- excessive glow
- neon edge lighting
- random gradients
- lens flares
- particles
- sparks
- smoke without context
- glowing rings
- holographic interfaces
- floating glass panels
- glossy UI cards
- excessive reflections
- heavy HDR
- exaggerated blur
- decorative 3D objects
- generic blue-purple cinematic lighting

If the design becomes weak after removing effects, improve the concept and composition instead of adding more effects.

Materials must behave naturally.

Do not make every surface glossy, metallic, translucent, chrome, plastic, or perfectly smooth.

Premium quality should come from typography, spacing, proportion, photography, texture, lighting, restraint, and compositional confidence.


9. PHOTOGRAPHIC AND COMPOSITING REALISM

The final artwork must look like a professionally assembled and retouched Photoshop advertising composite—not one fully synthesized AI scene.

Use:

- believable photographic elements
- accurate product cutouts
- clean masking
- natural edge separation
- consistent perspective
- believable scale
- realistic contact shadows
- ambient occlusion
- matching light direction
- matching shadow softness
- coherent depth of field
- matching sharpness
- consistent color temperature
- realistic reflected and bounced light
- environmental color contamination
- natural surface variation
- restrained grain
- subtle photographic imperfections

Avoid:

- floating subjects
- pasted-in products
- inconsistent lighting
- impossible perspective
- universal sharpness
- over-smoothed surfaces
- plastic textures
- synthetic perfection
- over-blended edges
- excessive retouching
- artificial cinematic lighting without context


PEOPLE

Use people only when they materially strengthen the message.

When people appear, they must look genuinely photographed.

Preserve:

- natural facial proportions
- realistic skin texture
- believable hair
- anatomically correct hands
- authentic posture
- relaxed expressions
- realistic fabric folds
- correct scale and perspective
- lighting consistent with the environment

Avoid waxy skin, beauty-filter faces, artificial eyes, perfect plastic teeth, malformed hands, generic stock poses, exaggerated expressions, unnatural gestures, and excessive rim lighting.


BACKGROUND

Backgrounds must feel like real photographed locations, practical studio sets, credible architectural environments, restrained graphic spaces, or carefully constructed Photoshop composites.

Use recognizable spatial structure, believable materials, coherent perspective, realistic depth, natural light falloff, and purposeful environmental details.

Avoid undefined visual mush, fake bokeh, impossible architecture, generic luxury interiors, random blurred objects, empty gradients without design purpose, or futuristic environments unrelated to the message.


10. PRODUCTS AND OFFICIAL ASSETS

Use supplied official products, packaging, logos, people, and source assets accurately.

Preserve:

- product proportions
- packaging design
- labels
- official colors
- visual identity
- important details
- material behavior
- correct orientation

Do not invent:

- products
- packaging
- claims
- prices
- statistics
- certifications
- partner logos
- proprietary devices
- fake dashboards
- meaningless interface labels
- unsupported product features

Do not redesign official packaging to improve the composition.

Products must feel physically present through correct scale, perspective, contact shadows, ambient occlusion, reflected light, matching sharpness, and coherent color grading.

Use 3D only when clearly supported by the brief, brand, product, or reference.


11. REFERENCE AUTHORITY

Study every attached reference and artifact directly.

Use each artifact only for its stated role.

Liked references define the target standard for:

- composition quality
- information density
- typography behavior
- negative space
- visual rhythm
- crop logic
- image-to-text relationship
- color discipline
- material treatment
- effect restraint
- campaign confidence
- finishing quality

Extract the design behavior and creative thinking of the references without copying their exact artwork.

Pay attention to:

- what the reference deliberately leaves empty
- how it creates hierarchy
- how typography interacts with imagery
- how it uses real objects or photography
- how it creates interest without excessive effects
- how the composition directs attention
- how the brand appears without overpowering the idea

Do not average conflicting references into a safe generic style.

Select the reference behavior most relevant to the current message.

Previous brand advertisements may guide factual accuracy, product treatment, official colors, logo usage, and brand recognition, but do not automatically copy their layout density, feature strips, badges, icon rows, CTA style, or promotional structure.


12. MOBILE-FIRST QUALITY

Evaluate the artwork at approximately 320–390 px wide.

At that size, the viewer must immediately understand:

- the main visual idea
- the exact headline
- the brand or product
- the intended action

The viewer should not need to zoom.

If the artwork feels dense, simplify it.

If the image and headline already communicate the message, remove unnecessary supporting elements.

If an effect does not improve understanding, realism, or hierarchy, remove it.

If typography is oversized because the visual concept is weak, improve the concept rather than enlarging the text.
