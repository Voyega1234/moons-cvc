COMPOSITION AND INFORMATION DENSITY

Build the artwork around no more than three primary visual groups:

1. main visual or primary typographic gesture
2. supporting message when genuinely necessary
3. brand and action area

Create one dominant focal point.

Use scale, crop, contrast, light, shadow, texture, repetition, or typography to establish hierarchy.

Let the composition breathe.

Keep at least one substantial quiet area free from unnecessary objects, copy, icons, badges, or decoration.

Do not distribute many small elements evenly across the canvas.

Do not display every feature, benefit, product variation, caption detail, or selling point in one artwork.

Supporting copy is optional unless explicitly marked mandatory.

Information that is not necessary for immediate understanding should remain in the caption.

Do not shrink typography or introduce small labels merely to fit more information.

If more than three areas compete for attention, simplify the artwork.


TYPOGRAPHY AND SPACING

Typography must feel intentionally designed as part of the composition.

Use:

- one primary font family
- no more than two font weights unless required by the official brand identity
- one dominant headline
- natural line breaks
- comfortable leading
- generous outer margins
- clear separation between text groups
- strong scale contrast
- clean alignment
- mobile-readable sizing

Typography should feel spacious, confident, and premium.

Do not fill every available area with text.

Do not compress the headline into a dense block.

Do not reduce line spacing only to make the copy fit.

When the exact headline is long, give it more compositional space and reduce surrounding elements.

Avoid:

- dense paragraphs
- several competing headlines
- excessive bold text
- narrow compressed type
- outlined typography
- heavy drop shadows
- too many text colors
- decorative emphasis on every keyword
- tiny product labels
- generic text panels added only for convenience
- overcrowded headline stacks

Typography may become the main visual device when this produces a stronger campaign design.

PHOTOSHOP-STYLE COMPOSITING

The final artwork must look like a premium advertising composite assembled and retouched in Photoshop using real photographic elements.

It should not look like one fully synthesized AI-generated scene.

Use:

- authentic photography
- realistic product cutouts
- clean masking and edge separation
- believable compositing
- natural photographic texture
- controlled professional retouching
- realistic contact shadows
- matching light direction
- matching sharpness and depth
- consistent color temperature
- coherent film grain or image texture
- believable reflections and bounced light

Every major subject should feel visually distinct yet naturally integrated into the same artwork.

Preserve slight photographic imperfections where appropriate.

Avoid excessive smoothing, perfect synthetic surfaces, and over-blended edges.


PEOPLE AND HUMAN REALISM

Use people only when they materially strengthen the message.

When people appear, they must look like real photographed humans.

Use:

- natural facial proportions
- realistic skin texture
- visible but refined pores
- believable hair strands
- natural hands and fingers
- authentic posture
- relaxed expression
- realistic fabric folds
- lighting consistent with the environment
- appropriate scale and perspective

Avoid:

- waxy skin
- overly smooth faces
- exaggerated expressions
- uncanny symmetry
- artificial eyes
- perfect plastic teeth
- malformed hands
- generic stock poses
- floating or pasted-in people
- AI-generated beauty-filter appearance


BACKGROUND AND ENVIRONMENT REALISM

Backgrounds must feel like real photographed locations, practical studio sets, or carefully constructed Photoshop composites.

Use:

- recognizable environmental structure
- believable surfaces and materials
- realistic depth
- natural light falloff
- controlled focus
- coherent perspective
- visible but restrained background detail

Avoid:

- undefined visual mush
- excessive fake bokeh
- dreamy synthetic interiors
- random blurred objects
- over-smoothed backgrounds
- impossible architecture
- inconsistent perspective
- generic gradient backgrounds without a design reason

The background should support the main visual rather than compete with it.

At that size, the viewer must immediately understand:

- the main visual idea
- the exact headline

- the intended action

If the artwork feels dense, simplify it.

If the image and headline already communicate the message, remove unnecessary supporting elements.

Do not add decorative information merely to make the composition appear complete.
